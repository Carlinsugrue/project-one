Professional Profficiency

Meeting Attendance

4/5

With the application of remote desktop to my personal machine and the majority of my subgroup assigned tasks relating only to myself, my presence within the project room was rather unnecessary. But I made sure that being present at the Monday and Thursday meetings was of paramount importance. Below can be found photos and recordings obtained from these congregations.

Communication With Peers

4/5

Although my group consisted of just me usually, there where several times when communication with peers was necessary, I believe in these instances I performed with an aura of professionalism. I must however admit that when I found myself in a tight predicament I would not continue to ask pertinent questions and would instead try to resolve the matter myself, such as the discussion of CodeLingo functionality to its respective staff. This would ultimately result in inefficient time management.

Project Documentation

3.5/5

My documentation throughout the project has unfortunately left much to be desired, as I struggled with CodeLingo software for many consecutive weeks I had failed to record what in particular was happening and in which week, leaving my timeline less robust than I would have hoped.

Adaptation to problems and changing requirements

4/5

Most everything that I have worked upon this semester has been foreign to me, be it the implementation of virtual machines such as Xibo digital signage within vSphere to the strange complexities of CodeLingo. There have been tasks within this project that I have been able to achieve relatively fast and competently, although there have been many instances of problems where I cannot simply google the question. It was a very frustrating experience and it mentally taxed me some days, but I felt that through it all I did well to find potential issues and solutions.

Technical Proficiency

Code Quality

3/5

Due to my projects nature, I haven’t particularly written much code, although I have produced several scripts for the lingo-builder server and the website itself. My strongest technical capabilities come from my Unix knowledge and OS understanding, which helped greatly in the BIT Platform group. Because of my chosen study path leaning more towards the world of systems administration my coding abilities have left my PHP/JavaScript pages suboptimal but now without spirit!

Following Development Best Practices

3/5

I feel that when it comes to best practices, I try my best to make things I develop simple and user friendly whilst also testing in a few various environments. A good example may be me always implementing new updates for CodeLingo software such as the platform or lingo binaries on my own home machine by just utilising VMWare Workstation. I can then diagnose any potential problems in a well understood environment before pushing to the servers located at OP. However, I understand that one of my largest downfalls is not regularly using industry recognised version controls such as git.

Version Control

3/5

Not the best. I recognise that I implemented proper version control a bit late. As when I was developing my projects it was only me utilising the code or software, so I only employed the 2 separate backups in 2 different locations approach. Usually I would have my software stored on my personal machine, external hard drive and project machine but as the end drew near I started to employ the use of git.

Overall Contribution

4/5

Perhaps it was just the hectic semester but I felt I put a good amount of effort into working on assignments passed to me, It may be a different thing altogether as to how much I was able to produce from this effort. And It’s hard to distinguish how much my work could influence the BIT Platform just yet, as I haven’t fully implemented / understood how powerful the CodeLingo workflow could be when integrated into the BIT Platform Pipeline. It has some great Potential, and I hope that in the advent I may pass project one I could continue to develop upon a proper toolset for lecturers and students.
